CD-13 Group Augmented Abstract

Estimating Regional Carbon Exchange using Aircraft and Surface Observations

Jielun Sun -- National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR)
Paulo Artaxo -- Universidade de Sao Paulo

Project Summary   

This proposal is in response to the NASA NRA-00-OES-08 on Carbon Cycle Science and on LBA ecology. Currently, long-term variations of atmospheric carbon dioxide are monitored by an array of flux towers, such as AmeriFlux towers over North America and EuroFlux towers in Europe. This long-term observational network covers a variety of biomes and diverse geographic areas. However, whether these towers capture the net carbon budget in the regions surrounding the towers and how we can scale-up these tower measurements to regional or continental scales are still challenging issues. We propose to identify and quantify carbon sources and sinks on the regional scale (around 100 km), and study their temporal variability by using coordinated ground-based and aircraft measurements with novel observational strategies. We propose to use either the Wyoming King Air or NCAR C-130 aircraft at two experiment sites: the existing WLEF 450-m tower in northern Wisconsin and the Large Scale Biosphere-Atmosphere (LBA) Experiment in Amazonia.      

We will apply different strategies for measuring the daytime and nighttime regional CO2 exchange. For the daytime regional CO2 exchange, we will directly  measure the CO2 turbulent flux close to the ground ecosystem. In addition, we will investigate the CO2 budget by measuring not only the temporal and spatial variations of the CO2 concentration, but also the advection of CO2, the CO2entrained at the top of the atmospheric boundary layer, and the CO2turbulent transport. For the nighttime regional CO2, we will fly repeatedly along a flight track during the early morning transition period to measure the CO2 flux accumulated through the night. Although focused primarily on CO2, we will also survey  spatial and temporal variations of the isoprene eddy flux as part of the regional carbon study. To quantify the regional CO2 transfer during the daytime, and to improve numerical modeling of the CO2 budget, we propose to use isoprene as a tracer to measure the entrainment velocity at the top of the atmospheric boundary        

The measured regional CO2 and isoprene fluxes will be related to satellite land classifications and topography data to estimate CO2 and isoprene fluxes on larger scales. By comparing the measured regional CO2 fluxes from the aircraft with the tower measurements at the two sites, we propose to improve the accuracy of the long-term observation of CO2 exchange from the AmeriFlux network and the tower network in Amazonia to represent regional CO2 budgets.
