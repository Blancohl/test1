The objectives of our participation in the Hydrological Component of the LBA program are:
 
Provide information on radiative fluxes that are required for advancing the understanding of the role of water in land-atmosphere interactions; this will include the total shortwave radiative fluxes, as well as Photosynthetically Active Radiation ( PAR) and Near-Infra-Red (NIR) radiation. This information will be provided at both boundaries of the atmosphere, namely, at the top of the atmosphere and at the earth's surface. 

Develop improved techniques for providing such information, that take into account deforestation and biomass burning in the Amazon Basin. Availability of shortwave radiative fluxes at both boundaries of the atmosphere will allow to estimate the contribution of the smoke to the solar heating of the atmospheric column.

Initially, we will provide historical information on surface and top of the atmosphere radiative fluxes, both on global and regional scales, to help the modeling community to synthesize their current hydrological models, and improve their capabilities to test scientific issues of concern to the Hydrology Program of the LBA. Historical radiative fluxes will be produced as part of an ongoing EOS PATHFINDER activity. Under the current project, they will be prepared to meet the needs of the LBA investigators.

New activities will be initiated to improve existing capabilities to derive surface and top of the atmosphere radiative fluxes, by incorporating unique conditions that exist over the Amazon Basin, in particular, in respect to deforestation and biomass burning; implement these improved methodologies at spatial and temporal scales of interest to the hydrological and ecological modeling community. This aspect of our activity will be directly related to actvities anticipated during the LBA experiment. It will also utilize results from the Ecology Component of the LBA Project.

Specifically, we plan to improve the characterization of surface conditions in the radiative retrieval scheme, accounting for effects of deforestation, and include better detection of smoke from biomass burning, and its modeling in terms of aerosol effects. The "ground truth" on surface radiative fluxes will be collected by independent investigators and used to test our algorithm in this unique environment, where high quality ground truth is almost non-existent.  In the context of the list of priorities of the LBA Hydrology Program, the information that will result from this project will enable to address the following issues of the program:

 Priority topic 1: Global scale hydrometeorology
evaluation of land surface parametrization, and the implications for global climate and weather forecast models

Priority topic 2: Continental and regional scale hydrometeorology
 
estimation of the surface hydrological and energy budgets at regional and continental scales 

modeling and prediction of the surface hydrological and energy budgets of the LBA region on time scales from diurnal to interannual

development and testing of improved algorithms to predict surface radiation budgets that take into account deforestation in Rondonia and information on biomass burning in the Amazon Basin

documentation of regional and continental energy and water cycles within the LBA
Priority topic 3: Mesoscale hydrometeorology
enhancement of he understanding of mesoscale convective and/or land surface processes

Priority topic 4: Regional and continental scale surface hydrology
demonstration of the use of remotely sensed hydrometeorological variables in connection with models of land surface fluxes.
